Wild Oysters Reefs

Climate change's effects on wild oyster reefs and how oyster reefs can help us stop it

by alex bui

Disappearing oyster reefs

Humanity, as a species, has had a large negative impact on many ecosystems and the lives of many other living things on our planet. Our dispersal of greenhouse gasses has caused more CO2 than ever to dissolve into the oceans, poisoning marine life and destroying our beloved sources of wild seafood.1

Oysters, in particular, are being harmed by acidification due to large amounts of dissolved CO2. Carbon dioxide reacts with water to create weak carbonic acid, stopping oyster larvae from creating their shells and dissolving the shells of other shellfish as well.3 Natural oyster reefs are disappearing fast because of ocean pollution, and do not show any signs of recovering. America's favorite bivalve mollusk is disappearing, and fast.3

Many efforts are being made to rebuild oyster reefs and other communities in American gulfs. We are off to a good start, but it's not nearly enough.2

How oysters can help us

Oysters as a species are very helpful to the whole ocean ecosystem. They clear out pollution, protect wildlife by filtering and eating parasites and other organisms, and also act as protection against coastal erosion.2 Oysters and other filter feeders are also great CO2 filterers, acting as carbon sinks when working together in giant oyster reefs.2

Protect wildlife
Oysters consume parasites that may harm fragile fish, plants, and animals, helping endangered species grow without having to worry about their microscopic predators. They help creatures around them flourish and can also act as homes for fish who need them.2

Pollution removal
A single oyster can filter up to 50 gallons of water a day in a suitable habitat. Excess nitrogen, carbon dioxide, and other harmful chemicals are taken in by oysters and are dumped on the sea floor harmlessly. As water clarity improves in specific habitats, seagrasses, fish, and other animals start coming back, bolstering water bodies and rebuilding ecosystems. 2

Coastal erosion
Because oysters tend to stick together, they can dampen the force of waves that would otherwise reach shores and erode beaches. Where oysters are, coasts are being protected against rising sea levels and precious beach sand is being saved. Groups in the Chesapeake Bay and Bangledesh are currently installing large concrete barriers for oysters to attach to. Alone, these barriers dampen waves as well, but oysters strengthen their power and have many other positive effects as well. Reefs can also grow to enormous sizes, building and building to water level. If oysters are used for coast protection, they will not have to be added upon later on.2

In conclusion,

A lot of other aspects of the ocean are being harmed by the abnormally low pH levels. Sea floors are being degraded, animals like coral are experiencing massive die offs, and almost every single organism is experiencing negative changes in some way. However, planting filter feeders in large coastal cities may be the solution to many of our water and beach related climate change problems.1, 3
